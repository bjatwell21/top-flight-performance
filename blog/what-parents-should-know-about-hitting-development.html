What Parents Should Know About Hitting Development

I talk to parents every week. And I want to be honest about the things that matter and the things that don't — because there's a lot of noise out there, and I've seen families make expensive mistakes chasing the wrong things.

Here's what I actually tell the parents who come in and ask me how to think about this stuff.

Frequency Isn't the Same as Development

More lessons doesn't automatically mean more improvement. I've seen kids take two lessons a week for a year and barely move. I've seen others train intentionally for a few months and completely transform. The difference isn't how often they showed up — it's whether they had a structured system that built on itself. When you're evaluating a program, ask: is there a plan? Does each session connect to the last one? If the answer is no, you're just buying hours.

Exit Velocity Isn't the Whole Picture

Exit velo is a useful data point. I like tracking it. But I've worked with hitters who have high exit velo and can't hit in games, and hitters with average velo who are dangerous at the plate because their approach and swing decisions are sharp. Development means improving the whole hitter — not just one metric. Be wary of anyone who sells you on a number without showing you the full picture of how your athlete is growing.

Age Matters for What You Focus On

A 12-year-old and a 17-year-old need completely different things. Younger athletes need to build athletic foundations and develop feel for the swing — loading them up with too many mechanical cues early is counterproductive and can actually slow down development. Older athletes who are approaching the college or pro level need to be sharpening specific things quickly and developing a clear identity at the plate. A good coach knows the difference and adjusts accordingly. At TFP, we build programs around where the athlete actually is, not a generic template.

Ask What Happens Between Sessions

This is one of the most important questions you can ask any hitting instructor: what does your athlete do between sessions? If the answer is nothing — if there's no programming, no structure, no accountability — you're paying for an hour a week and leaving the other 167 hours to chance. At TFP, every athlete has a complete program through the TrainHeroic app. Development doesn't stop when the session does, and that consistent work in between is often what makes the biggest difference over a full season.

Trust the Process, Not the Highlight

The best thing you can do as a parent is resist the urge to chase quick results. Baseball is a long game. The athletes who develop the fastest and go the furthest are the ones who commit to a consistent, intentional process over time — not the ones who bounce from facility to facility chasing a trend. That's what we're building at TFP. Not a shortcut. A system.

If you have questions about where your athlete is and what a real development plan looks like, come in. We'll give you a straight answer.
